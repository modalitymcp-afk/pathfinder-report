Opening

Foreword

Digital transformation in health and care is no longer a question of whether technology can be adopted. The harder question is whether it can help us change how care is organised, delivered and experienced.

At Pathfinder, our work has always been rooted in practical improvement: bringing clinicians, system leaders, innovators, patients and partners into the same conversation, testing ideas against real delivery, and asking what it would take for change to become part of routine care rather than another pilot.

The 2026 Digital Transformation Summit was built around a simple but demanding proposition: digital is not the reform itself. It enables reform when it is connected to redesigned pathways, workforce models, governance, infrastructure, data and funding. That distinction matters. Across the NHS and wider health and care system, we have many examples of tools going live. What we need now is the discipline to ask whether those tools are improving access, reducing burden, strengthening safety, supporting equity, enabling care closer to home and giving people and staff back time.

One of the most encouraging aspects of the summit was the breadth of that conversation. More than 850 people attended, and colleagues joined online from beyond the UK, including from South Africa and Vietnam. Although this report is firmly rooted in UK NHS reform, that international interest was a useful reminder that many of the practical challenges are shared across health systems: access, workforce pressure, public trust, data quality, implementation capacity, and the need to make technology serve relationships and pathways rather than the other way round.

This report reflects the discussion at the summit, the feedback from colleagues across the system and the wider direction of reform: shifting more care from hospital to community, moving from analogue to digital, and strengthening prevention. It is not a formal evaluation. It is a system leadership report, intended to help boards, ICBs, provider organisations, clinicians, technology partners and policymakers move from digital adoption to operating model transformation.

My hope is that it is useful because it is practical. It argues for ambition, but also for sequencing; for innovation, but also for governance; for AI and automation, but only where the foundations are strong; for patient-facing digital routes, but not at the expense of staff workload or public trust.

If there is one message I would want readers to take away, it is this: the future of digital health will not be shaped by the organisations that buy the most technology. It will be shaped by those willing to redesign work, invest in people, build trust, measure impact and stop doing the things that no longer add value.

I am grateful to everyone who contributed to the summit and to the review of this report. The challenge now is to turn these insights into implementation.

Prof Vincent Sai

Group CEO & Partner

Opening

Executive summary

Health and care has reached a more demanding phase of digital transformation. The task is no longer simply to adopt tools, but to redesign the operating model around care: how pathways work, how teams use time, how information flows, how risk is governed and how impact is measured.

This report is written primarily from a UK NHS perspective, drawing on Pathfinder’s 2026 Digital Transformation Summit, structured inputs, external review and wider sector discussion. More than 850 people attended the summit, with strong UK participation and international interest, including from South Africa and Vietnam. Those international contributions are not treated as a separate comparative evidence base; they show that the core questions resonate beyond the NHS.

The central message is deliberately simple: digital is not the reform itself. It enables reform when it is embedded into redesigned pathways, workforce models, governance arrangements, data infrastructure and funding mechanisms. Without those conditions, technology can digitise existing pressure, create additional work and make transformation appear more advanced than it really is.

The argument aligns with the current direction of NHS reform: shifting more care from hospital to community, moving from analogue to digital, and strengthening prevention through neighbourhood, population-health and proactive care models. Neighbourhood health is used as a practical example because it shows why digital transformation must be treated as an operating capability, not a standalone toolkit. Shared datasets, aligned care plans, risk stratification, patient navigation, cross-setting visibility and multidisciplinary team coordination only create value when connected to clear accountability and redesigned work.

The practical implications are clear:

Problem before procurement

systems should define the operational, patient, workforce and equity problem before selecting, extending or buying technology.

Pathway redesign before scale

digital should support substitution, simplification and stopping old work, not sit on top of unreformed processes.

Implementation as a discipline

time, training, onboarding, fallback routes, super-user support and feedback loops determine whether technology reduces or creates burden.

Infrastructure as reform capacity

primary and community care need hardware, connectivity, integration, data infrastructure, estates and implementation capacity if the shift to care closer to home is to be credible.

Impact over activity

success should be judged by flow, workload, safety, equity, substitution, patient experience, productivity and value, not by usage alone.

Governance at scale

AI, automation and data-driven care need clinical safety, information governance, cybersecurity, patient information, consent, checking functions and clear accountability.

Trust and inclusion

digital transformation depends on public confidence, transparency, meaningful engagement and evidence that benefits reach people with different needs, languages, confidence levels and access barriers.

The report also highlights the difference between adoption pushed through procurement and adoption pulled by clinicians and teams because it gives time back. Tools that scale well tend to make work safer, easier or more effective for frontline users. Tools that add checking, logging, duplicate entry or workaround activity may increase digital activity without creating transformation.

A recurring risk is the funding cliff edge. Pilots may show value, but still fail if no recurrent funding, governance and business-as-usual route has been agreed from the outset. Funding should therefore support implementation, substitution, data quality, pathway redesign and decommissioning of old work, not only technology purchase.

Artificial intelligence is treated as a destination, not a shortcut. AI and automation should be scaled where the operating model, workforce, data, governance and assurance foundations are in place. The priority is augmentation with built-in checks: clear points where outputs are verified, escalated, audited and made transparent, so that the system remains safe, contestable and clinically accountable.

The stakeholder implications are clear:

For boards and system leaders, the question is whether digital investment is changing care delivery, releasing capacity and improving outcomes.

For commissioners, the test is whether funding routes support implementation, substitution and recurrent value rather than short-term pilots.

For providers and frontline teams, the priority is to start with practical problems and design digital change around workflow, safety, training and staff experience.

For technology partners, the expectation is to align products to real-world pathways, interoperability, clinical safety, equity, usability and measurable outcomes.

For patients and communities, digital transformation should improve access, experience, continuity, agency and trust, not simply shift responsibility onto individuals.

The conclusion is simple: the next phase should not be judged by how many tools are adopted, but by whether services are redesigned, staff are supported, risks are governed and outcomes improve. The practical challenge is to move from digital adoption to system transformation.

Section 01

Purpose and evidence base

A strategic synthesis for health and care leaders.

This report is intended as a strategic synthesis for health and care leaders. It is deliberately grounded in the UK NHS context, while recognising the wider resonance of the questions explored. It draws on Pathfinder’s 2026 Digital Transformation Summit, structured workshop and structured inputs, international online participation, and wider sector discussion on neighbourhood health, digital access, AI, automation, interoperability, governance, workforce readiness and implementation at scale.

It is not a formal policy evaluation, academic evidence review or statistically representative research report. The inputs are used as directional insight to test and strengthen the central argument: digital transformation will only create system-level value when it is embedded into redesigned pathways, workforce models, governance arrangements, data flows and funding mechanisms.

It is best read as a practical contribution to the next phase of health and care reform. It seeks to clarify the conditions required for digital tools to support genuine service transformation, rather than simply accelerating existing pressure or digitising unreformed processes.

The practical implication is that technology decisions should follow design thinking: understand the service problem, redesign the pathway, decide what work can be stopped or substituted, and only then select, extend, repurpose or procure the technology needed to support the change.

01

Why now

Digital tools are widely adopted, but measurable system-level transformation remains inconsistent.

02

Core problem

Technology is often implemented faster than operating models are redesigned, and too often procured before service design, workflow redesign and implementation conditions are clear.

03

Purpose

To clarify the sequencing, priority domains and practical non-negotiables required to implement the digital, data and technology vision in the NHS 10 Year Plan and translate it into transformation at scale.

Evidence note

Summit and workshop inputs are used as directional stakeholder insight. They help shape emphasis and sequencing, but should not be read as statistically representative survey evidence.

Section 02

Who this report is for

The practical implications are different for each stakeholder group.

This report is intended for a broad health and care audience, but its central message is directed at those responsible for turning digital ambition into practical service change.

System leaders and ICBs

Commissioners

Technology suppliers and innovation partners

Align products to real-world pathways, interoperability, clinical safety, equity, workflow burden and measurable outcomes rather than adoption alone.

Policymakers, regulators and improvement partners

Support the standards, infrastructure, governance, assurance and funding routes needed to move from pilots to sustainable system infrastructure.

Patients, citizens and communities

Section 03

Digital transformation in context

Connecting national policy, summit learning and system pressure to practical delivery.

This report offers a practical response to the named direction of health and care reform in the NHS 10 Year Plan: shifting more care from hospital to community, moving from analogue to digital, and strengthening prevention through neighbourhood, population-health and proactive care models. The central test is whether digital tools help systems redesign work, release capacity, improve productivity, personalise care, value people's time and improve outcomes, rather than simply digitising existing pressure.

What the summit tested

From event output to strategic synthesis.

The summit provided a structured opportunity to test a working hypothesis with a cross-sector, primarily UK NHS audience, alongside colleagues joining online from overseas: digital adoption alone is insufficient. Transformation depends on whether digital capability is embedded into redesigned operating models, workforce practice, governance, data flows and funding.

The discussion was strengthened by the breadth of perspectives in the room and online, including primary care, hospital clinicians, voluntary and community sector colleagues, technology partners, academic contributors and wider system leaders.

The discussions broadly reinforced this hypothesis. They also sharpened the sequencing: problem definition before procurement, pathway redesign before scale, workforce readiness before expectation, governance before uncontrolled automation, and impact measurement before claims of transformation.

A system at an inflection point

Beyond any single organisation.

Health and care systems are entering a more mature phase of digital transformation. The early focus on digitisation, access tools and platform adoption has delivered important gains in efficiency, visibility and engagement. However, structural pressures - workforce constraints, rising demand, fragmented accountability and increasing complexity of care delivery - require a more integrated transformation approach.

Transformation is no longer confined to a single institution, organisation or national system. It is shaped by a wider ecosystem of providers, commissioners, regional leaders, technology partners, regulators, educators, local government, voluntary and community organisations, patients and communities. This means digital transformation should be understood as a system architecture challenge, not an IT deployment exercise.

Section 04

Neighbourhood health, people and practical readiness

Making digital transformation work for neighbourhood models, patients and staff.

Neighbourhood health is used here as a practical example because it illustrates why standalone technology will not deliver reform. Neighbourhood working depends on community-centred care, strong general practice, shared data, cross-setting visibility, clear accountability and leadership across primary care, community services, mental health, local authorities, social care and acute providers.

The digital layer in neighbourhood models should therefore be described less as a set of tools and more as an operating capability. Shared datasets, aligned care plans, risk stratification, population health analytics, patient-facing navigation, cross-setting visibility and multidisciplinary coordination are practical foundations for integrated neighbourhood working.

Problem-led and human-supported change

The practical counterweight to abstract transformation language.

01

Start with a problem

The strongest examples of digital change start with a defined frontline, patient, community or operational problem, not a technology-first solution.

02

Design for inclusion

Digital inclusion requires help with devices, confidence, connectivity, navigation, translated content and multi-modal resources.

03

Evidence equity over time

Equity needs to be designed into digital health from the start and evidenced over time, not treated as a late-stage mitigation or communications claim.

Examples discussed through the summit reinforced that the most useful digital change starts with a practical problem and is judged by implementation discipline and measurable impact, rather than by supplier profile or tool adoption alone.

This should include a deliberate focus on functions and processes, not products. In some cases the right answer will be to optimise, extend or repurpose existing technology rather than buy something new.

Patient and staff readiness

Digital change must work for both patients and frontline teams.

Patients, citizens and communities must remain central to digital transformation. Digital models should improve access, experience, safety, continuity and agency, rather than simply shifting responsibility onto individuals or assuming that all patients can navigate digital routes equally.

However, patient-facing barriers should not be treated as the only, or always the primary, constraint on progress. Digital exclusion, privacy concerns, confidence and usability remain important, but the pace and quality of transformation are often limited just as much (if not even more) by staff capacity, workflow design, training, governance, infrastructure and the usability of tools for frontline teams.

01

Patient-facing readiness

Access, confidence, privacy, language, connectivity, digital literacy, usability, trust, inclusion and agency.

02

Staff-facing readiness

Capacity, workflow, training, governance, infrastructure, tool usability, safety, feedback routes and reduction of avoidable burden.

The practical aim is not to choose between patient readiness and staff readiness. It is to design for both. If digital change improves the patient front door but creates additional checking, logging, triage burden or workaround activity for staff, it is unlikely to deliver sustainable transformation.

Section 05

Barriers and enabling conditions

From uncomfortable truths to the policy, infrastructure and governance conditions needed for scale.

Digital is not the reform itself

Digital can enable a new operating model, but it cannot compensate for unreformed pathways, unclear accountability, underfunded implementation or fragmented incentives.

Shiny tool syndrome is still real

Systems can be pulled towards buying what the market offers before defining the operational problem, agreeing outcomes or designing implementation.

Transformation fatigue is a system signal

Fatigue should not be interpreted simply as resistance. It may reflect too many projects, poor sequencing, limited training, unclear benefits and tools that do not remove legacy work.

Digital can create failure demand

Digital activity should reduce avoidable work, not create extra checking, logging, reporting, duplicate entry, safety monitoring or workarounds because underlying processes remain unreformed.

Equity-washing is a risk

Digital tools should not be presented as inclusive without evidence that they reach, support and benefit people facing language, access, confidence, connectivity or complexity barriers.

Infrastructure underinvestment limits reform

Primary and community care cannot be expected to deliver a serious shift to digital and community-based care without capital investment in hardware, connectivity, integration, data infrastructure, estates and implementation capacity.

Systemic barriers to meaningful transformation

Structural conditions that technology procurement cannot solve on its own.

The barriers identified across the review are systemic and cannot be resolved through technology procurement alone. Culture and adoption matter, but they should not be over-used as explanations for limited progress. The more fundamental issues are capacity, infrastructure, incentives, safety, implementation conditions and system-level governance.

A recurring risk is the funding cliff edge: a successful pilot may demonstrate better experience, fewer avoidable admissions or reduced workload, but still fail if no recurrent funding, governance and business-as-usual route has been agreed from the outset.

Culture and trust also need to be treated as conditions for readiness. Historic experience of change, scepticism about previous programmes, public concern about data use and staff fatigue can all shape whether a digital model is accepted and safely adopted.

Workforce capacity and training

Transformation needs funded time, role-specific training, confidence-building, leadership support, psychological safety and fallback arrangements.

Infrastructure investment

The shift to community and digital requires serious investment in primary/community care infrastructure, not only procurement of digital tools.

Interoperability and data silos

Pathway-level integration depends on shared data, usable records, common standards, supplier alignment and funded integration capability.

Governance, IG and clinical safety

AI, automation and data-driven care require clinical safety, human oversight, cybersecurity, liability awareness, risk management and clear accountability.

Funding and commissioning models

Funding must support implementation, substitution, pathway redesign, decommissioning of old work and recurrent value, not only pilots or technology purchase.

Misaligned incentives

Organisations need incentives to collaborate, share learning, remove duplication and decommission superseded tools.

Digital inclusion and equity

Digital models must be designed for people with different needs, languages, confidence levels, connectivity constraints and access barriers.

Public trust and perception

Digital transformation depends on public trust, transparent use of data and technology, meaningful engagement, patient information and clear routes for consent, opt-out and challenge.

Infrastructure, interoperability and governance as policy priorities

The enabling architecture for digital and neighbourhood reform.

Infrastructure investment is a core requirement for digital and neighbourhood reform. If policy is serious about shifting care from hospital to community and from analogue to digital, digital infrastructure in primary and community care cannot continue to be treated as secondary or discretionary. Hardware, connectivity, estates, integration capability, data infrastructure and implementation capacity are all part of the operating model required for neighbourhood health.

Care closer to home also needs a workforce and commissioning model. Remote consultation, distributed multidisciplinary working and proactive community support require training, supervision, role design and a clear commissioning route, not technology alone.

Integration also cannot be achieved through aspiration alone. It requires funded interoperability infrastructure, shared standards and practical alignment with the systems already used at scale across primary care, including major clinical system suppliers. National and regional leaders should therefore treat integration capability as a core enabling investment, not as a local workaround to be solved repeatedly by individual providers.

Impact measurement depends on data that is often incomplete, inconsistently coded or difficult to link across the primary, community and secondary care interface. Data quality and linkage therefore need to be funded as part of transformation, not treated as a later evaluation task.

There may also be an opportunity to consider whether some data governance, information governance and clinical safety functions could be scaled more consistently at national level. Where appropriate, clearer national data-controller arrangements or shared assurance routes could reduce the need for the same governance tasks to be repeated thousands of times for each digital solution - or, worse, inconsistently undertaken or not completed at all.

For AI and automation, assurance should also include augmentation and checking functions: defined points where outputs are interrogated, verified, escalated and audited so that automation remains transparent, contestable and clinically safe.

Design principle

Governance should be rigorous, proportionate and scalable, not needlessly duplicative, and should include patient-impact assessment, patient information, consent and ongoing monitoring where risk changes over time.

Section 06

From adoption to transformation: implementation route

The practical route from tools to operating model change, sequencing and readiness.

The practical question for boards, ICBs, provider collaboratives and technology partners is not whether a digital tool has gone live, but whether the operating model around it has changed.

Adoption that is pulled by clinicians and teams because the tool gives time back behaves differently from adoption pushed through procurement. The test is whether frontline users can see a burden removed, not just a platform deployed.

Practical sequence for transformation

Technology at scale comes after the operating conditions are designed.

Before defining the solution, systems should listen to users and understand the local drivers of the problem: where demand arises, where work is duplicated, where patients wait, where staff rework occurs and where risk is being carried.

1

Define the problem

The priority problem and its drivers are understood through operational data, patient and staff listening, workflow observation and equity insight before a solution is selected.

2

Redesign the pathway

Roles, handoffs, escalation routes, decision points and stopping rules are redesigned around the work that needs to happen.

3

Prepare the workforce

Staff have protected time, role-specific training, super-user support, feedback routes and psychological safety.

4

Build foundations

Data quality, interoperability, governance, clinical safety, IG, cybersecurity, business continuity and funding are in place.

5

Apply technology at scale

Technology is introduced as part of the redesigned operating model, with implementation support and decommissioning plans.

6

Measure impact and substitution

Success is assessed through flow, workload, safety, equity, experience, productivity, substitution and recurrent value.

Implementation quality as the missing discipline

A practical architecture for making digital change work.

Implementation quality now matters as much as, and often more than, the sophistication of the technology. The recurring frontline needs are time, training, communication, support, confidence, basic equipment, psychological safety, fallback arrangements and clarity on the purpose of change.

Implementation should therefore include continuing evaluation after go-live, including whether the tool is still delivering value, operating safely, supporting consent and information needs, and reducing rather than displacing workload.

What implementation architecture requires

Clear problem definition and pathway redesign before procurement or rollout; staff involvement, staged onboarding, feedback loops and funded time for adoption; clinical safety review, data-quality planning, business continuity and fallback arrangements; decommissioning plans and measurable substitution benefits.

Section 07

Non-negotiables for sustainable system transformation

Practical tests for whether digital is creating transformation rather than additional activity.

The non-negotiables are grouped below to make them easier to use as an assurance checklist. The test is whether digital change is safe, useful, equitable and capable of scaling without adding hidden work.

Safety, governance and accountability

Core assurance conditions must be designed into the model, not added after deployment.

  • Clinical safety, information governance, cybersecurity and liability routes are clear.
  • Human oversight, checking functions, escalation and audit are built in.
  • Assurance is rigorous, proportionate and scalable across deployments.

People, workforce and adoption

Digital should make work safer and easier for staff while keeping patients and communities central.

  • Workforce readiness is funded through time, training, onboarding and support.
  • Tools reduce duplicate entry, extra checking and workaround burden.
  • Patients, citizens, communities and human judgement remain central.

Data, infrastructure and interoperability

Transformation depends on trusted data and shared infrastructure, not isolated tools.

  • Operational data supports timely intervention, not only retrospective reporting.
  • Primary and community care infrastructure is treated as a core reform enabler.
  • Shared records, APIs and pathway-level visibility support cross-setting work.

Outcomes, equity and implementation

Transformation should be judged by measurable value and the ability to sustain change.

  • Start with real-world problems and measurable outcomes before selecting technology.
  • Measure impact, safety, equity, substitution and experience, not adoption alone.
  • Funding, procurement and commissioning support implementation effort and recurrent value.

Section 08

Strategic outlook and next phase

The next chapter of transformation.

The next phase of digital transformation will likely be characterised by the convergence of AI, automation and workflow redesign; greater emphasis on measurable system impact; movement from pilots to embedded infrastructure; deeper cross-sector collaboration; and a stronger focus on execution discipline over innovation volume.

Future work should also test the public-facing legitimacy of digital transformation: whether people understand how data and AI are being used, whether they can exercise meaningful choice, and whether engagement improves trust rather than simply explaining decisions already made.

The defining shift will not be the introduction of new technologies alone, but the integration of digital capability into core system architecture: clear problems, redesigned pathways, prepared workforces, trusted data, safe governance, interoperable infrastructure and funding that supports implementation.

Practical action frame

Individual organisations cannot solve all systemic barriers alone. They can, however, identify one practical change to make, one barrier to escalate or remove, and one partnership to strengthen, while contributing to the wider system conditions required for transformation.

01

One change to make

A practical change that removes friction for patients, staff or pathways.

02

One barrier to remove

A capacity, governance, interoperability or funding constraint that is holding transformation back.

03

One partnership to strengthen

A relationship across provider, commissioner, technology, academic or community boundaries.

Implementation tool

Implementation question set

Use these questions before moving from ambition to procurement, pilot or scale. They are deliberately practical and should be answered in plain operational language.

Starting problem

  • What patient, staff or pathway problem are we solving?
  • What old work will stop if this succeeds?
  • Who has shaped the problem from lived user insight?

Operating model

  • What pathway, role, decision and escalation changes are required?
  • Where will work shift, and who will absorb it?
  • What is the fallback if the digital process fails?

Workforce and adoption

  • Is there protected time for training and onboarding?
  • Who are the super-users and local champions?
  • How will psychological safety and feedback be handled?

Data and intelligence

  • What operational data will trigger intervention in real time?
  • Which measures show impact beyond usage?
  • How will equity, access and experience be monitored?

Safety and governance

  • Are clinical safety, information governance, cybersecurity and liability clear?
  • What human oversight and checking functions are required?
  • Who owns escalation, audit and assurance?

Scale and value

  • What recurrent cost and implementation effort are needed?
  • What benefits are cashable, capacity-releasing or quality-improving?
  • What must be true before this scales beyond the first team or site?
